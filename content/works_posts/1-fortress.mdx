      - content: >
          Historically, the features and components on Castlery’s eCommerce site
          have been created and maintained on an on-demand basis without a UX
          team - the creative team designs visuals, and the frontend developers
          determine the requirements and hard-code these features. This led to
          inconsistent shopping experiences and UI, low web accessibility
          awareness, a “popup hell”, and long time to market for features and
          improvements. This was the situation I faced, as part of a newly hired
          2-person UX team.


          On one hand, we believed that shopping experiences should be
          consistent, intuitive, and on-brand regardless of our customers’
          device type. On the other, we wanted our developers to have a more
          efficient and effective way of developing and maintaining the
          eCommerce site across 3 markets. So, together with a senior frontend
          developer, we created the first design system for Castlery - the
          Fortress Design System, from the ground up.

    title: "O1. Fortress: Starting From Zero"
    showSectionTitle: true
    blocks:
      - size: sm
        _template: spacer
      - content: >
          With no design system or any existing documentation of site elements
          and visuals, I began by conducting an internal audit of all existing
          elements and components before categorizing them into their respective
          component types and variants. I found:


          * Dozens of buttons styles, incorrectly applied font weights, and
          single-use icons everywhere

          * Many areas failing accessibility standards

          * Different component types either serving the same purpose or used
          incorrectly

          * Missing components
            * For example, toasts and popovers did not exist; all feedback indicators were popups
        _template: richTextContent
      - size: sm
        _template: spacer
      - content: >
          Next, I started working on the foundational elements before moving
          onto streamlining and building actual components for the site. I cross
          referenced the brand guidebook with an existing CSS style sheet from
          the front end developers, then narrowed down colour use, identified
          utility colours, established naming conventions, set up tokens and
          variables, etc. It was an iterative and time consuming process to:


          1. Recreate and/or streamline the components before reviewing it with
          the front end developers

          2. Piece the components into key high fidelity screens and/or
          prototypes (wherever needed) in mobile and desktop views to
          communicate and review the design and functionality with key
          stakeholders who were not versed in UI creation

    title: "O2. Structure, Scalability"
    showSectionTitle: true
    blocks:
      - size: sm
        _template: spacer
      - content: >
          The Fortress Design System was intentionally divided into its
          Foundational and Component libraries to futureproof the design system.
          This structure ensures that Fortress can be scaled up and expanded for
          future implementations into other systems that Castlery uses, namely
          the POS system and the management platform. These platforms have
          different interfaces, users, and user needs that differ from the
          general e-Commerce website.


          The foundation library is set up with tokens and variables, from which
          the eCommerce component library mainly draws from. As the design
          system scales up, future component libraries serving the POS and
          management system will also be able to draw from the same foundational
          library, ensuring atomic consistency across all platforms. (More
          information about the component library is in the section above.)

    title: "O4. Talking Business: Value of Design"
    showSectionTitle: true
    blocks:
      - size: sm
        _template: spacer
      - content: >
          It was difficult to advocate for UX in a company that mainly sees UX
          as UI. Teams typically do not involve UX in their user flows, system
          flows, or service blueprint education until they need an interface
          done.


          Without a senior or lead UX designer to learn from and shadow, one of
          the hardest things I had to learn and perform on my own (as the UX
          designer responsible for Fortress) was communicating the value of
          design and translating this into impact for business stakeholders’ buy
          in.


          As such, I had to find a way to show how a largely UI project like
          Fortress can provide tangible impact to the company. I spoke about
          design systems at product monthlies, called out Fortress updates, and
          showed demos of what the completed pages would look like with
          Fortress.


          Ultimately, I came up with a roadmap for Fortress that involved not
          just construction, but also implementation, rollout in phases, and
          even the migration of hard-coded pages into [modular CMS
          pages](https://frn-cne.vercel.app/works/3-storyblok). This was
          particularly tricky given that I had limited visibility on other
          initiatives, but I managed to sort it out by working with the Product
          Head to identify any overlaps.
