        blocks:
          - content: |
              Since conception, Castlery's websites (across 3 markets) have always been designed and developed in an adaptive way - once for mobile and another for desktop. As such, users with device sizes that deviate from the preset sizes (e.g. tablets/wider mobile screens) would get subpar experiences on the e-commerce site. The adaptive design meant that tablet users were completely neglected.

              The company however, started to shift their focus to becoming mobile-first, with an increasing number of users accessing and purchasing from the site. There was also a growing interest in supporting tablet experiences as the sales teams 1. disliked the current POS system and 2. found it hard to show customers things in the showrooms on tablets (all sales usually done via tablet), and the company started to take a greater interest in creating online-to-offline (and vice versa) experiences. This was a huge chance to push for the shift from adaptive to responsive design.

        title: O1. Responsive Web Evangelism Is Sometimes About Timing
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              When I had just joined Castlery, their adaptive design was an issue I had identified early, but it was hard to push the business agenda for adopting responsive web design.

              Only in 2023 did the company shift its focus to mobile-first as the numbers were showing increased mobile access and revenue. This gave me the opportunity to start advocating and having conversations about the value of mobile-first and device-accessibility, which aligned with other ongoing initiatives around offline-to-online and vice-versa (O2O) and omnichannel experiences.

              I also managed to get buy-in and commitment from Product and Tech teams to drive the initiative. I was paired with 2 front-end developers to overhaul the website, which was a lot of resource for a small company.

      - anchorId: design_build
        title: O2. Designing & Building For Responsiveness
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              As the only UX designer on this initiative, I audited existing web breakpoints and experiences and extracted device metrics on users visiting the site. From there, I collaborated with the front-end developers to identify the breakpoints that we should be supporting.

              I also happened to be the only UX designer creating and maintaining the Fortress design system, giving me the agency to define what the responsive site should look like on mobile, tablet, and desktop. This was done by prototyping layouts of key site pages, documenting it Fortress, and reworking any components in the component library that needed to be responsive.

              I also provided some training for the other UX designer, teaching him how to turn his existing designs responsive (Figma had just released min-max breakpoints then, which made my job easier).

      - anchorId: define_success
        title: O3. Defining Success For Structural Initiatives
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              For an extremely data-driven company, translating impact, measuring success and reporting on metrics was a core part of each initiative. Hence, while it is easy for Tech, UX, and Product to see the value in moving to responsive design, it was harder for non-tech-related teams to see its value and the opportunities it will create.

              This was a huge challenge for me as I now had to define and measure what success is for responsive web design - something that does not directly impact day-to-day business or profit. Here, I sought help from a colleague who worked on structural initiatives. Ultimately, the definition and impact measurement at launch was done in 2 ways:

              1. Roadmap of short- and long-term initiatives across the business that responsive design has and will support and enable. This was done jointly with the Product head and Tech lead. It justified the resource investment into responsive design and increased trust in the UX, Product, and Tech teams.
              2. Immediate quantitative indicators of success - getting metrics for the most-impacted target group (tablet users) and direct internal stakeholders (developers and designers) as a result of launch. We were able to quickly see improvement in experiences for tablet users through a significantly reduced bounce rate and increased engagement rate per session across all 3 markets. For internal stakeholders, the design and build time was reduced by at least 25%.
