              To produce the project’s first UX-based user findings and data, we established an operational baseline through a dual-track research initiative: fieldwork and baseline testing.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **3-month Fieldwork and Shadowing in an Active Airbase**

              I participated in fieldwork, shadowing diverse user personas from the different user groups during their daily duties over 3 months. Then, together with the team, charted individual workflows to understand the various roles, their interactions and hand-offs with other users within and across functional groups, and device usage.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Baseline Research of the Legacy System on Ops Day**

              To observe the existing legacy system under operational pressure, we ran an observational field assessment during the airbase's quarterly Operations Exercise Day (Ops Day). As this was a baseline assessment of the legacy system, there was no deployment of new code; we simply <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">mapped our research objectives against the war-scenarios to evaluate existing shortcomings, track information lag, and see exactly where capabilities broke down under stress</mark>.

              *(Context: On Ops Day, multiple airbases participate in different scenarios designed to test the bases’ operational readiness and performance)*

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Co-Design Workshopping**

              We wrapped up our baseline research by running Airbnb’s “11-Star Framework” as a design exercise workshop with selected users to co-design their ideal, frictionless future system. <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">Pushing them past the perceived limitations of their existing system allowed us to uncover latent operational needs</mark>, which we merged with our hard field observations to structure our design direction.

              **Workflows, Service Blueprint**

              With the baseline research complete, we spent a month synthesizing the observational data into detailed User Journey Maps, workflows, and a comprehensive Service Blueprint. This living document established our central source of truth, mapping out:

              * **User Touchpoints**: When a user transitions from a desktop environment (S3/Commanders doing planning and delegation) to a mobile device (Ground troopers executing tasks).
              * **Command Chains**: Mapping user reporting structure, and how information flows under normal vs. high-stress conditions

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Key UX Insight: Role-Based Personas vs. Consumer Archetypes**

              Unlike conventional UX, <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">defense systems reject traditional consumer archetypes - users are bound by their functions, resulting in role-based personas being created instead of consumer-facing archetypes</mark>. Within a military setting, users are expected to perform their roles, and difference in choices are due to user expertise and situational context. They focus on operational environments, cognitive load under stress, and strict physical and security requirements. Thus, all our users do not, and cannot, fit into personas such as ‘budget bob, who is motivated by discounts’.

              For example:

              * All troopers are expected to carry out their duties in executing tasks regardless of their preference
              * All commanding officers are expected to sensemake on-ground situations and delegate troopers to de-escalate issues, based on the objectives/directives set by a base commander

              As such, the system that we design will need to <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">help our users quickly arrive at decisions for mission success and operator survivability</mark>.

              **Strategic Roadmap Management**

              With a 10-year project scope, the design team work with our Product Managers and user-stakeholders every 3 months to prioritize upcoming development blocks. The design team operates 3 months ahead of the technical teams to ensure all engineering sprints begin with pre-validated assets. When business owners are unavailable, we liaise with designated proxy-owners to keep requirements grounded in real-world airbase operations.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Bi-Weekly Formative Testing Framework**

              Because military environments limit user access to strict bi-weekly windows, we maximize these short periods through targeted testing methodologies before any designs are committed to code:

              Conducting UX work in a military environment comes with a strict constraint: We are only granted access to users once every two weeks. As such, to maximize these bi-weekly windows, utilized targeted usability testing methods:

              * **Card Sorting**: Validates macro-navigation and information hierarchy across all modules
              * **A/B Testing**: Resolve contentious UI patterns within the design team using objective user data
              * **Wireframe & Prototype Testing**: Executed at a granular level to answer functional questions (e.g., "Is it intuitive for a ground trooper to report a fault on mobile?").

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Guerrilla Testing (The Fallback)**

              Whenever we require additional validation rounds outside of our bi-weekly user windows, we use Guerrilla Testing. We would adapt the exact same methodologies (card sorting, A/B testing, and prototype evaluations) to run it internally with proxy users to keep design momentum moving forward without stalled timelines.

              **The Structural Roadblock: The Illusion of Isolated Testing**

              Initially, we tested our new modules and features in isolation (e.g., creating and resolving a fault in the Inventory Management module). However, this did not reflect real-world usability. In an active airbase, tasks are continuous, chaotic, and highly interdependent. <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">This focus on feature creation and testing ultimately compelled us to pivot towards a live operational testing approach that encompasses all modules</mark>.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Going "Live" with the Test-System on a Second Ops Day**

              To measure true operational usability, our Product Managers negotiated with stakeholders to deploy our new system test environment during another quarterly Ops Day exercise. I worked with our UX Architect (UXA) and another designer to map our test system's capabilities directly against the scenarios, tracking data dependencies as information flowed between mobile and desktop states.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Increasing Fieldwork Opportunities through the Developers**

              Because our design team could not cover the massive scale of an active airbase alone, we onboarded and trained our developers to act as field observers. I conducted a session on how to take objective observation notes and collect timestamped data without interfering with the ongoing military exercise. This training was crucial to ensure that the <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">data collected remained unbiased, standardized, and focused on user friction and workflow bottlenecks rather than code-level assumptions</mark>.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Execution & Data Collection**

              On this second Ops Day, the entire project team was strategically deployed across the airbase to shadow the new system across 3 environments:

              * (Desktop) Within a high-stress command center serving alignment and providing sensemaking
              * (Desktop) Within functional group centers
              * (Mobile) On-ground monitoring ground personnel executing tasks

              **Cross-Functional Triaging: Strategic Prioritization in Action**

              By analyzing our insights through this two-dimensional lens, we removed subjectivity from the product roadmap, allowing for a <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">data-driven framework</mark> that allowed design and developer teams to align priorities and actions.

              Another added bonus of this system-level validation came from synchronizing user behavioral data directly with infrastructure performance metrics. By working together with the tech teams, we could use our timestamped user-friction data points to cross-reference the exact second an operator experienced friction against backend server logs. This helped us look past standard human error and match real-world operational bottlenecks against technical limitations, such as database latency, API timeouts, and server spikes under heavy load. The tech teams will then be able to pinpoint and implement targeted improvements on the infrastructure side.
