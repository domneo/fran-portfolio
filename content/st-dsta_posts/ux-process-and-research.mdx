              ### Key insight: role-based personas, not consumer archetypes

              Unlike conventional UX, <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">defense design reject traditional consumer archetypes - users are bound by their functions, resulting in role-based personas being created instead of consumer-facing archetypes</mark>.

              Within a military setting, users are expected to perform their roles, and difference in choices are due to user expertise and situational context. They focus on operational environments, cognitive load under stress, and strict physical and security requirements. Thus, all our users do not, and cannot, fit into personas such as ‘budget bob, who is motivated by discounts’.

              This reframed every decision: we weren't designing for preference but for mission success. A failing interaction can mean mission failure.

              ### The illusion of isolated testing

              Module-level results looked good. Put together under real operational conditions, they revealed failures isolated testing had never surfaced - so we pivoted to live, system-wide testing during a quarterly Ops Day.

              <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">Isolated testing fails not because the method is wrong, but because real tasks don't respect module boundaries.</mark>

              I worked with our UX Architect (UXA) and another designer to map our test system's capabilities directly against the scenarios, tracking data dependencies as information flowed between mobile and desktop states.

          - col1: |
              ### What I'd do differently

              I'd train developers as observers earlier - the data quality jumped once they learned to watch for user friction instead of code behaviour, and we only unlocked that at the system-validation stage.

              I'd also invest sooner in living documentation that captured not just what we decided, but why, and what we chose not to pursue.
            col2: |
              ### What I'm still uncertain about

              The prioritisation matrix worked as an alignment tool, but its durability across planning cycles is still being tested.

              Also, the process was built for a high-stakes, resource-constrained environment - how much of it transfers to faster commercial contexts without losing the quality of insight?
            _template: twoColumn_1_1
          - _template: divider
          - content: |
              ### On the hardest alignment moment

              The hardest conversation was: time > methodology

              Justifying 3 months of fieldwork plus a month of synthesis before any design work meant reframing the missing baseline as a liability, not a research gap: every decision made without it would need revisiting later.

              **Doing the research upfront was cheaper than doing it as remediation.** That argument landed because it was framed in the leadership's own terms of risk - that's my hypothesis.
