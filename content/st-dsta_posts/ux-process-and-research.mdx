              This case study documents the ongoing transformation of a legacy defense system serving four major operational functional groups and a central commanding group across multiple airbases. Designed for a high-stakes environment with interconnected desktop and mobile capabilities, the existing legacy software was outdated and failing to keep pace with modern operations.

              When I (and the team) joined this decade-long initiative, we faced an immediate roadblock: the legacy system had extremely little research and user findings documented. There was no baseline understanding of user behavior under stress or how information flowed between systems.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Process & Research Challenges**

              * Establishing a baseline
                * Build the project’s very first repository of user workflows, operational hand-offs, and behavioral data
                * Translate complex military command chains, multi-device hand-offs into structured UX artifacts
              * Shifting from Feature-Isolation to System Interoperability
                * Moving away from traditional, siloed testing methodologies to validating how multiple complex modules actually worked together under real-world conditions
              * Objectively justify design decisions
                * Build a data-driven framework to supplement the project’s 10-year roadmap, ensuring design priorities were backed by empirical field data rather than subjective stakeholder opinions

              <spacer size="sm" />

              So in order to build a comprehensive, logically sound ecosystem, we had to establish a rigorous, repeatable UX process. <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">We adopted the UX Double Diamond framework, execution-testing it at both the module and system levels to validate foundational architectures before layering on dependent features.</mark>

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **My Roles**

              * As the On-Project Design Lead, I oversee the design system and UI implementation across the entire product.
              * As a UX Designer, I directly own and lead four foundational modules: Access Controls, Integrated Map, Inventory Management, and Scheduling Management

        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              To produce the project’s first UX-based user findings and data, we established an operational baseline through a dual-track research initiative: fieldwork and baseline testing.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **3-month Fieldwork and Shadowing in an Active Airbase**

              I participated in fieldwork, shadowing diverse user personas from the different user groups during their daily duties. Then, together with the team, charted individual workflows to understand the various roles, their interactions and hand-offs with other users within and across functional groups, and device usage.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Baseline Research of the Legacy System on Ops Day**

              To observe the existing legacy system under operational pressure, we ran an observational field assessment during the airbase's quarterly Operations Exercise Day (Ops Day). As this was a baseline assessment of the legacy system, there was no deployment of new code; we simply mapped our research objectives against the war-scenarios to evaluate existing shortcomings, track information lag, and see exactly where capabilities broke down under stress.

              *(Context: On Ops Day, multiple airbases participate in different scenarios designed to test the bases’ operational readiness and performance)*

              <spacer size="sm" />

              **Co-Design Workshopping**

              We wrapped up our baseline research by running Airbnb’s “11-Star Framework” as a design exercise workshop with selected users to co-design their ideal, frictionless future system. Pushing them past the perceived limitations of their existing system allowed us to uncover latent operational needs, which we merged with our hard field observations to structure our design direction.

              With the roadmap set, the team began designing the modules. As a UX designer, I took direct ownership and designed the system's foundational pillars:

              Testing in a military environment comes with a strict constraint: We are only granted access to users once every two weeks. To maximize these bi-weekly windows, we utilized targeted usability testing methods:

              **Card Sorting**: As the Design Lead, I partnered with the UX Architect to run card sorting sessions, validating the macro-navigation and information hierarchy across all modules

              **A/B Testing**: Used to resolve contentious design patterns within the team or with stakeholders before committing to code

              **Wireframe-Prototype Testing**: Executed at a granular level to answer functional questions, such as: “Is it intuitive for a ground trooper to create an 'Issue' on mobile?” or “Does our new 'Access Controls' concept intuitively reflect their real-world chain of command?”

              Where we require results quicky and have no access to users, we relied on **Guerilla Testing**.

      - anchorId: systempivot
        title: '04. Phase 4: The System-Level Pivot (From Granular to Operational)'
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              Once individual modules were deployed into a test environment, we hit a roadblock. <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">Testing modules and features in isolation (e.g. creating an asset fault and resolving it within Inventory Management module) did not reflect real-world usability.</mark> In an airbase, tasks are not isolated; they are continuous, chaotic, and highly interdependent. Ad-hoc testing was giving us a false sense of security.

              **Going "Live" on Ops Day**

              To get an accurate picture, we requested for an actual trial with the test-system (developed modules that reside in a test environment - the final product will only be handed off once the 10 years is up). The Product Managers managed to negotiate with the users to deploy our test-system during the airbase's quarterly Operations Exercise Day (Ops Day) - day with multiple simulated war-game scenarios involving all airbases, designed to test operational readiness.

              This allowed for a chance to see our designs come to life, understand the benefits and shortcomings of the designed capabilities, and to measure the system's usability against as-real-as-possible conditions. Could users operate the software under stress? Did the modules interact seamlessly to produce the required operational results?

              **Preparation & Alignment**

              To pull off a high-fidelity system trial during an active military exercise, I collaborated closely with our UX Architect (UXA) and the designated testing Person-in-Charge (POC) to map the system's technical capabilities directly against the users’ specific war-game scenarios. This step was crucial to pinpoint exactly which module would be deployed, which user groups would interact with it at any given minute, and how data dependencies would flow between the desktop and mobile environments.

              Recognizing that <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">operational usability goes hand-in-hand with technical reliability</mark>, we decided to integrate the tech teams’ expertise into the testing process. We wanted to look past standard user friction and simultaneously capture hard system performance metrics - such as API latency, data sync speeds, and system responsiveness under high-stress, heavy-load conditions.

        title: '05. Phase 5: Execution, Data Collection'
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              **Training Non-Designers for UX Fieldwork**

              Because of the massive scale of the airbase, our design team could not be everywhere at once. To bridge this gap, we onboarded and trained our developers to act as field observers.

              I conducted a session on how to take objective observation notes and collect timestamped data without interfering with the ongoing military exercise. This training was crucial to ensure that the data collected by engineers remained unbiased, standardized, and focused on user friction and workflow bottlenecks rather than code-level assumptions.

              **Ops Day: Capturing Data in the Fog of War**

              On Ops Day, the entire project team was strategically deployed across the airbase's operational ecosystem.

              We split into groups of 3 to shadow various functional groups in real-time:

              * (Desktop) Within a high-stress command center serving alignment and providing sensemaking
              * (Desktop) Within functional group centers
              * (Mobile) On-ground monitoring ground personnel executing tasks

              As the war-game scenarios played out, we captured a continuous stream of timestamped interaction data, user errors, bugs, and technical performance lags. Over the course of the exercise, this collaborative effort allowed us to harvest <mark style="background-color: #BFDBFE">over 600 unique observation data points across 40 selected users in 25 unique roles at 7 locations</mark>, giving us an unprecedented, unvarnished look at system usability under true operational pressure.

              Executing a thorough UX process for an enterprise-scale ecosystem meant that we cannot design in isolated modules, nor can it rely on quick, superficial fixes. From the findings and my experience here, I realise that we need the time to map, test, and iterate on highly interdependent modules where a single design change in one area can trigger a ripple effect across the entire system.

              While the process is undeniably long, we have come to realise that its rigor is exactly what allows us to justify our design decisions to stakeholders and tech teams.
