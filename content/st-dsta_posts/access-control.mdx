          - content: |
              ### Taking complexity out of the user's hands

              Manual permission-setting is where access systems go wrong - it's the source of most user error and security gaps. I worked with a small user group to fix the rights for each archetype, then had developers hardcode them.

              This was a deliberately rigid approach to solve a problem that's easy to miss until the system won't boot.

              <divider />

              ### The Bootstrap Paradox

              * No Super Admin defined → the system can't propagate a chain of command.
              * No roles with owner-type rights → the modules are unusable, so the system can't run.

              <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">You can't use the system to set up the permissions it needs in order to be usable.</mark>

              The way out was a hardcoded master logic in the backend. This provided a guaranteed baseline that makes the System operational from the moment of deployment, before anyone configures anything.
            _template: oneColumn
          - _template: spacer
          - image: /bootstrap.png

          - content: |
              ### Why rigidity was chosen

              Hardcoding rights goes against the instinct to keep everything configurable. However, configurability at the foundation is exactly what creates the bootstrap trap.

              **The base layer and the user layer can be seen as two separate problems**:

              1. The base is rigid, invisible, and guaranteed correct (i.e. source of truth)
              2. The flexibility users need lives one level up, in the Org Chart

              That separation let me give users drag-and-drop freedom without ever exposing the logic that keeps the system alive.

              #### What I'd do differently

              I'd get the hardcoded archetype rights in front of users earlier, even in rough form. Rights defined on paper feel different once they shape what a real operator can and can't see.

              ### The Hardest Call

              The hardest decision was committing to hardcode the foundation, in a project whose whole brief was flexibility.

              Making the case meant reframing it: <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">the rigidity was the precondition for customisation in other parts of the system</mark>. Without a guaranteed base, there's no stable ground to customise from.

              Once stakeholders saw the base and user layers as separate problems, the trade-off stopped feeling like a compromise and started feeling like the architecture.
