              #### What is the System?

              The system is a defence operational platform that serves two distinct user groups: command-level personnel who need rapid situational awareness, and operational staff who execute cross-module tasks under time pressure.

              The system was built module by module - each solving a specific problem in isolation. By the time users needed to work across them, the cost of that architectural decision had become a daily operational burden.
            col2: |
              #### My Role

              I was the sole designer responsible for the end-to-end redesign. That meant everything from convincing stakeholders the side panel needed to go, to running the internal workshop, defining the component library, stress-testing interactions against user roles, and validating the design before handoff.

              I also contributed reusable components back to the organisation's broader Design System (DS).

              ### Problem 01:  Information was scattered across modules

              Users had to navigate between separate modules to assemble a coherent operational picture — causing constant context loss and fragmented decision-making.

              <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">For operational teams, context loss isn't inconvenient. It's a direct cost to response time.</mark>
            _template: oneColumn
          - _template: divider
          - content: |
              ### Problem 02:  Every action required excessive clicks

              The side panel was the primary interaction point for everything — features were built in isolation, resulting in workflows that forced users to switch modules to complete a single task.

              <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">Tedious module-switching raises cognitive load and increases time-to-resolution across all user groups</mark>
            _template: oneColumn
          - _template: divider
          - content: |
              ### Problem 03:  The map was a display, not a workspace

              Users consistently drew parallels to strategy games — they wanted to forage for information directly on the map, not search a panel to find it. The current map wasn't built for that.

              <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">A map that can't be interacted with directly isn't an operational tool — it's a dashboard. The two have different cognitive costs.</mark>

              ### What were users *really *asking for?

              Recurring feedback pointed clearly to the map. Users drew parallels between their workflows and strategy and management-type games — they wanted the map to work like that: directly manipulable, information-rich, responsive.

              This wasn't a feature request. It was a signal that the underlying mental model of the system — map as display, panel as workspace — was wrong. The redesign needed to invert that relationship entirely.

              One recurring feedback we received from users centers on the System’s map interface. However, any deviation from the broader organisation’s design system was difficult. The Project was supportive of the change, but I had to accomplish the following for change to happen:

              1. Identify map-reliant modules that could be integrated into the Map
              2. Overhaul existing map interactions to handle the integrated modules
              3. Validate the changes against the current System
                 * Prove our users right - a "game-inspired" map could actually reduce cognitive load and interaction costs

        title: 'O3. Defining Map Elements, Components, & Interactions'
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              With the layout now in place, next was to meaningfully integrate the map-reliant modules into the map.

              I started by conducting a comprehensive audit of all features and functions within the 2 modules for a joint team workshop involving the UX Team, the Developer Team, and our 2 Product Managers. This workshop was done internally as **we do not have consistent and regular access to our users**.

              Our project teams came together to workshop the audited features and functions into an "ideal map". We brainstormed how to move away from side-panel dependency toward direct map manipulation. Through the workshop, we defined components, content, and required interactions. This resulted in a functional blueprint that ensured the new "discovery-based" experience was technically feasible and operationally sound.

              Based on the results of the internal workshop, I identified the specific components needed to support the new map-centric logic. These components were added to our project-level Design System, with reusable elements contributed back to the organization’s broader Design System.

              For each component, I defined guidelines on:

              * Design specifications, variants, placement logic, behavioral rules, etc.
                * Also established keyboard shortcuts for features and workflows
              * Underlying UX and HCI Principles that influenced the component
                * E.g. Cognitive load reduction, Fitts’s Law, etc.
                * This was especially helpful for the UX Team in understanding the design intent, so that they can use the components to their full potential in their own designs
              * Possible types of integrated content from the map-reliant modules
                * Different types of content had to appear differently in the Integrated Map
                * Some content had to appear contextually across the map, while others are surfaced visually as supplementary information
