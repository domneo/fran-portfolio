              #### What is the System?

              The system is a defence operational platform that serves two distinct user groups: command-level personnel who need rapid situational awareness, and operational staff who execute cross-module tasks under time pressure.

              The system was built module by module - each solving a specific problem in isolation. By the time users needed to work across them, the cost of that architectural decision had become a daily operational burden.
            col2: |
              #### My Role

              I was the sole designer responsible for the end-to-end redesign. That meant everything from convincing stakeholders the side panel needed to go, to running the internal workshop, defining the component library, stress-testing interactions against user roles, and validating the design before handoff.

              I also contributed reusable components back to the organisation's broader Design System (DS).

              ### What were users \*really \*asking for?

              Recurring feedback pointed clearly to the map. Users drew parallels between their workflows and strategy and management-type games — they wanted the map to work like that: directly manipulable, information-rich, responsive.

              This wasn't a feature request. It was a signal that the underlying mental model of the system — map as display, panel as workspace — was wrong. The redesign needed to invert that relationship entirely.

              ### Design Judgment

              We didn't have consistent access to users - a constraint that we had to work with. I chose to lean into the internal workshop as a structured substitute: if I could get the people closest to both the product and the users in a room, align them on what the map needed to do, and have them challenge each assumption, the output would be more defensible than informal feedback alone.

              The risk I accepted: the workshop reflected the team's mental model of users, not users themselves. I held that uncertainty throughout the design process, and it's part of why the comparative validation at the end was non-negotiable.

              ### The first decision was structural

              Kill the side panel, expand the map. Everything else would follow from that one commitment - because once the map is the primary surface, every interaction has to be designed to live there.

              <spacer size="sm" />

              ### The constraint I had to navigate

              Deviating from the organisation's established design system required explicit justification - I couldn't just redesign the layout. I drew from the broader organisation's defined map layout as the foundation, then proposed project-specific enhancements on top of it. This meant every structural change I made had to be argued, not assumed. That slowed early progress but created artefacts - rationale documents, component annotations - that became essential during development handoff.

              ### Solution 01: Map as Primary Workspace

              <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">All information lives on the map.</mark>

              The map now fills the full canvas. Contextual panels surface on demand — anchored to the map, not competing with it. Users interact directly with map elements to access information, rather than searching a side panel to find it.

              No module switching required for any critical workflow — de-escalation, sense-making, and asset management all happen from the map itself.

              ### Solution 03: Comparative Validation

              <mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">Tested against people who hadn't touched the design</mark><mark style="background-color: #FEF08A">.</mark>

              Six developers (not involved in the workshop or map module) helped validate the new layout and interactions with paper prototypes. Three tested the current system, three tested the Integrated Map, with the same task set. The split was deliberate: I wanted to rule out familiarity as a variable.

              Results confirmed the core hypothesis: the new mental model worked. 50% reduction in workflow time. No module switching required for critical workflows. Cognitive load qualitatively lower across all test participants.

              ### What I'd do differently

              I'd push harder for even one round of direct user access before the workshop. The internal session was rigorous, but the workshop participants carried assumptions about operational workflows that I couldn't fully interrogate without someone from the field in the room. The comparative validation partially addressed this — but earlier, lighter-touch user contact would have caught at least one component decision I had to revise late.

              I'd also document the "why not" decisions more formally:

              * Features we scoped out
              * Layout directions we explored and rejected
              * Organisational constraints that shaped those calls.

              That institutional memory becomes critical as development extends across sprint cycles.
            col2: |
              ### What I'm still uncertain about

              The keyboard shortcuts. I argued for them, got alignment, and the data supports the decision, but we don't yet have longitudinal data on adoption after the initial learning period. I believe the sustained speed gain is worth the onboarding cost. I'm holding that as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.

              More broadly: this was an internal validation. The Integrated Map goes to real users during UAT later this year. That's when the assumptions we couldn't test,  particularly around the two distinct user groups operating simultaneously in the same workspace, will either hold or break.
            _template: twoColumn_1_1
          - _template: divider
          - content: |
              ### On getting the side panel removed

              The PMs were supportive of the map-centric direction in principle, but nervous about removing the side panel outright - it was the interaction model the whole system was built around. I had to make the case not just with user feedback, but with a structural argument: that keeping the side panel in any form would force every new component to choose between two competing interaction paradigms, and that inconsistency would compound over time.

              The move that unlocked alignment was the layout stress test: running every existing user workflow through both the side-panel model and the map-centric model side by side, and showing how many steps the panel version added per task. Once the PMs could see the cumulative cost across workflows rather than per-feature, the conversation shifted from "is this worth doing" to "how do we phase it".
