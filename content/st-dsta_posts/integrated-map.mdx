              To quickly understand consumer sentiments and perceptions, I proceeded with a convenience sampling of 26 shoppers at MUJI's flagship store in Singapore. These shoppers alternated between online and offline shopping.

              I found that:

              * An overwhelming 23/26 consumers believed they could shop on the MUJI SG website
              * All 26 respondents reported that the site's "look and feel" is strongly reminiscent of an e-commerce site
              * All respondents enjoyed shopping and browsing at MUJI's brick and mortar store

        title: Problem Statement & Reframing
        showSectionTitle: true

          - content: |
              #### Problem Statement

              Online shoppers want convenience and direct access to all products without having to visit MUJI's physical stores or 3rd party online retailers. However, they are unable to efficiently browse for products and make purchases directly from MUJI SG. They are confused by the existing navigation system as they spend time understanding how the website works instead of searching for products. They also assume that direct e-commerce is possible.

      - anchorId: info_architecture
        title: Building An Information Architecture
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              As MUJI has a large inventory of items and categories arranged in a way that was confusing for users, it had to be reorganised and streamlined for a better navigational experience.

              First, to find out what made sense to users, I conducted an open card sort with 4 users to understand:

              1. Users mental models - how they believe products should be grouped, and;
              2. SG consumer's nomenclature through how they label these groups

              What I found was that product organisation wasn't an issue, but instead **individual categories could be renamed to better reflect its ontology**.
