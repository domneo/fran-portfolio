              The project is a decade-long transformation of a legacy defence system. The existing software is outdated, failing to keep pace with modern operations.

              Because a defence system is an interconnected ecosystem with desktop and mobile capabilities, we had several challenges:

              1. Designing the developing right - what was prioritized and built would truly meet the users' needs, while remaining comprehensive and logically sound, and having foundational modules established and validated first before layered, dependent features are introduced
              2. Ensuring that individual modules built by different designers are seamlessly integrated into a unified experience
              3. Ensuring that users are able to effectively use the system and its various capabilities under high stakes, high pressure situations

              Within this project, I have two roles to play:

              1. As the On-Project Design Lead, I oversee the design system and UI implementation across the entire product.
              2. As a UX Designer, I directly own and lead four foundational modules: Access Controls, Integrated Map, Inventory Management, and Scheduling Management

              To tackle this massive scope, the design team adopted the UX Double Diamond framework, execution-tested at both the module and system levels.

        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              **3-Month Fieldwork & Shadowing**

              To understand the problems and user pains, we first spent three months conducting deep ethnographic research inside an active airbase. I participated in fieldwork, shadowing diverse user personas from the different user groups during their daily duties. During this process, we  charted individual workflows to understand the various roles, their interactions and hand-offs with other users within and across functional groups, and device usage.

              **Service Blueprint**

              After the fieldwork, it took a full month to synthesize the massive amount of observational data into comprehensive User Journey Maps, User Workflows, and Service Blueprints.

              This living document serves as a source of truth that mapped out:

              * User Touchpoints: When a user transitions from a desktop environment (S3/Commanders doing planning and delegation) to a mobile device (Ground troopers executing tasks).
              * Command Chains: Who reports to whom, and how information flows under normal vs. high-stress conditions

              **Workshopping**

              Additionally, we ran Airbnb’s “11-Star Framework” as a design exercise workshop with a selected group of users to co-design their ideal, frictionless future system. This was a good way to understand what the users would want beyond their current understanding of the system they use. We incorporated their wants with the observations and insights we learnt.

              **Key Learning Point**

              Unlike conventional UX, the personas we develop are constrained role-based personas instead of consumer-facing archetypes. Within a military setting, users are expected to perform their roles, and difference in choices are due to user expertise and situational context. They focus on operational environments, cognitive load under stress, and strict physical and security requirements. Thus, all our users do not, and cannot, fit into personas such as ‘budget bob, who is motivated by discounts’. 

              For example:

              * All troopers are expected to carry out their duties in executing tasks regardless of their preference
              * All commanding officers are expected to sensemake on-ground situations and delegate troopers to de-escalate issues, based on the objectives/directives set by a base commander

              As such, the system that we design will need to help our users quickly arrive at decisions for mission success and operator survivability.

              With the roadmap set, the team began designing the modules.

              In my role as a UX designer, I took direct ownership of the foundational pillars of the system:

              1. The Design System
              2. Access Rights
              3. Integrated Map
              4. Inventory Management

              The modules are written separately from the UX process as this page details the research and testing done at a system-level.

              Testing in a military environment comes with a strict constraint: We are only granted access to users once every two weeks. To maximize these bi-weekly windows, we utilized targeted usability testing methods:

              **Card Sorting**: As the Design Lead, I partnered with the UX Architect to run card sorting sessions, validating the macro-navigation and information hierarchy across all modules

              **A/B Testing**: Used to resolve contentious design patterns within the team or with stakeholders before committing to code

              **Wireframe-Prototype Testin**g: Executed at a granular level to answer functional questions, such as: “Is it intuitive for a ground trooper to create an 'Issue' on mobile?” or “Does our new 'Access Controls' concept intuitively reflect their real-world chain of command?”

      - anchorId: systempivot
        title: '04. Phase 4: The System-Level Pivot (From Granular to Operational)'
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              Once individual modules were deployed into a test environment, we hit a roadblock. **Testing modules and features in isolation (e.g. creating an asset fault and resolving it within Inventory Management module) did not reflect real-world usability**. In an airbase, tasks are not isolated; they are continuous, chaotic, and highly interdependent. Ad-hoc testing was giving us a false sense of security.

              **Going "Live" on Ops Day**

              To get an accurate picture, we requested for an actual trial with the test-system (developed modules that reside in a test environment - the final product will only be handed off once the 10 years is up). The Product Managers managed to negotiate with the users to deploy our test-system during the airbase's Quarterly Operations Exercise Day - a high-stakes, simulated war-games scenario designed to test operational readiness.

              This allowed for a chance to see our designs come to life, understand the benefits and shortcomings of the designed capabilities, and to measure the system's usability against as-real-as-possible conditions. Could users operate the software under stress? Did the modules interact seamlessly to produce the required operational results?

              **Preparation & Alignment**

              To pull off a high-fidelity system trial during an active military exercise, meticulous alignment was required. I collaborated closely with our UX Architect (UXA) and the designated testing Point of Contact (POC) to map the system's technical capabilities directly against the users’ specific war-game scenarios. This step was crucial to pinpoint exactly which module would be deployed, which user groups would interact with it at any given minute, and how data dependencies would flow between the desktop and mobile environments.

              Recognizing that **operational usability goes hand-in-hand with technical reliability**, we decided to integrate the tech teams’ expertise into the testing process. We wanted to look past standard user friction and simultaneously capture hard system performance metrics - such as API latency, data sync speeds, and system responsiveness under high-stress, heavy-load conditions.

      - anchorId: Execution
        title: '05. Phase 5: Execution, Data Collection'
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: |
              **Training Non-Designers for Fieldwork**

              Because of the massive scale of the airbase, our design team could not be everywhere at once. To bridge this gap, we onboarded and trained our developers to act as field observers.

              I conducted a session on how to take objective observation notes and collect timestamped data without interfering with the ongoing military exercise. This training was crucial to ensure that the data collected by engineers remained unbiased, standardized, and focused on user friction and workflow bottlenecks rather than code-level assumptions.

              **Ops Day: Capturing Data in the Fog of War**

              On Ops Day, the entire project team was strategically deployed across the airbase's operational ecosystem.

              We split up in groups of 3 to shadow various functional groups in real-time:

              * (Desktop) Within a high-stress command center serving alignment and providing sensemaking
              * (Desktop) Within functional group centers
              * (Mobile) On-ground monitoring ground personnel executing tasks

              As the war-game scenarios played out, we captured a continuous stream of timestamped interaction data, user errors, and technical performance lags. Over the course of the exercise, this collaborative effort allowed us to harvest **over 600 unique observation data points across 40 selected users in 25 unique roles at 7 locations**, giving us an unprecedented, unvarnished look at system usability under true operational pressure.

        showSectionTitle: true

              After the Ops Day, we had to transform our observations and data into structured, actionable insights, we digitized the fieldwork data and conducted an extensive affinity mapping session. These insights directly influenced our overarching design direction and product decisions, while simultaneously highlighting areas for technical optimization.

              Additionally, the timestamped data we captured during the exercise was handed over to the engineering team. By matching our user behavioral timestamps with the system’s backend logs, the developers could cross-reference exact moments of user friction against system limitations, server spikes, and database latency. This allowed the technical team to pinpoint and implement targeted improvements on the infrastructure side.

              To prioritize how we should execute design iterations and address technical gaps, we categorized every single insight across a two-dimensional matrix. I worked with the UXA and testing-POC designer to come up with the matrixes.
