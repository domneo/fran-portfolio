        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: >
              Historically, the features and components on Castlery's eCommerce
              site have been created and maintained on an on-demand basis
              without a UX team - the creative team designs visuals, and the
              frontend developers determine the requirements and hard-code these
              features. This led to inconsistent shopping experiences and UI,
              low web accessibility awareness, a "popup hell", and long time to
              market for features and improvements. This was the situation I
              faced, as part of a newly hired 2-person UX team.


              On one hand, we believed that shopping experiences should be
              consistent, intuitive, and on-brand regardless of our customers'
              device type. On the other, we wanted our developers to have a more
              efficient and effective way of developing and maintaining the
              eCommerce site across 3 markets. So, together with a senior
              frontend developer, we created the first design system for
              Castlery - the Fortress Design System, from the ground up.

          - content: >
              With no design system or any existing documentation of site
              elements and visuals, I began by conducting an internal audit of
              all existing elements and components before categorizing them into
              their respective component types and variants. I found:


              * Dozens of buttons styles, incorrectly applied font weights, and
              single-use icons everywhere

              * Many areas failing accessibility standards

              * Different component types either serving the same purpose or
              used incorrectly

              * Missing components
                * For example, toasts and popovers did not exist; all feedback indicators were popups

              prototypes (wherever needed) in mobile and desktop views to
              communicate and review the design and functionality with key
              stakeholders who were not versed in UI creation

      - anchorId: structure_scalability
        title: "O2. Structure, Scalability"
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: >
              The Fortress Design System was intentionally divided into its
              Foundational and Component libraries to futureproof the design
              system. This structure ensures that Fortress can be scaled up and
              expanded for future implementations into other systems that
              Castlery uses, namely the POS system and the management platform.
              These platforms have different interfaces, users, and user needs
              that differ from the general e-Commerce website.


              The foundation library is set up with tokens and variables, from
              which the eCommerce component library mainly draws from. As the
              design system scales up, future component libraries serving the
              POS and management system will also be able to draw from the same
              foundational library, ensuring atomic consistency across all
              platforms. (More information about the component library is in the
              section above.)

      - anchorId: business_value
        title: "O4. Talking Business: Value of Design"
        showSectionTitle: true
        blocks:
          - content: >
              It was difficult to advocate for UX in a company that mainly sees
              UX as UI. Teams typically do not involve UX in their user flows,
              system flows, or service blueprint education until they need an
              interface done.


              Without a senior or lead UX designer to learn from and shadow, one
              of the hardest things I had to learn and perform on my own (as the
              UX designer responsible for Fortress) was communicating the value
              of design and translating this into impact for business
              stakeholders' buy in.


              As such, I had to find a way to show how a largely UI project like
              Fortress can provide tangible impact to the company. I spoke about
              design systems at product monthlies, called out Fortress updates,
              and showed demos of what the completed pages would look like with
              Fortress.


              Ultimately, I came up with a roadmap for Fortress that involved
              not just construction, but also implementation, rollout in phases,
              and even the migration of hard-coded pages into [modular CMS
              pages](https://frn-cne.vercel.app/castlery/self-authored-content).
              This was particularly tricky given that I had limited visibility
              on other initiatives, but I managed to sort it out by working with
              the Product Head to identify any overlaps.
